Smoking, Drugs and Alcohol

During your teenage years, it’s pretty normal for you to find yourself in situations where cigarettes, drugs and alcohol are available. If you are going to try any of these, you should do so in moderation (know your limits!) and make sure you are with friends and people you who trust. And don’t ever do anything that you don’t feel comfortable with. Sometimes people smoke, drink or take drugs to escape from other problems they are having in their lives (stress, low moods, problems at home or at school).

Drugs are chemicals that you take into your body, which change the way you feel and act. There are lots of different types of drugs and they can all be harmful. Illegal drugs are described in different classes (A, B and C) depending on how unsafe they are. The harmful effects of these include feeling paranoid, depressed, dizzy, anxious, out of control or physically ill (vomiting). They can also make you feel 'high', relaxed, happy, confident and energetic. Talk to Frank is one of the most informative and well-known drugs and alcohol advice sites for young people.

Here are the KAOS team’s top tips to reduce your drug use or stop taking drugs:

  • Avoid places or situations where there will be drugs
  • Make a note of your drug use and set yourself little goals
  • Think about what makes you take drugs and how it would be talk about this
  • Get rid of the drugs you have by flushing them down the toilet
  • Find new hobbies and interest to take your mind off drugs
  • Find places in your area that might offer support and treatment

    • Drinking alcohol can be dangerous particularly when your body is still developing. Drinking alcohol can make you less aware of danger, so you’re more likely to hurt yourself. In the long-term, drinking alcohol on a regular basis increases the risk of developing a range of illnesses such as cancer, stroke, heart disease, liver disease and damage to the brain and nervous system.

    ‘Units’ are a simple measure to express the quantity of pure alcohol in a drink. The higher the ABV percentage you seen on the bottle, the stronger the alcohol and the more ‘units’ it will contain. In the UK, doctors advise that adult men and women should not drink more than 14 units of alcohol per week, roughly the equivalent of 6 pints of beer or 10 small glasses of wine. It’s good if you can have a few ‘alcohol free’ days and to avoid ‘binging’ (heavy drinking to deliberately get drunk) as this is linked to lots of health problems including liver damage, stomach ulcers and obesity. For information on units, https://www.nhs.uk/Livewell/alcohol/Pages/alcohol-units.aspx or you can check out videos of young people’s experiences of drugs and alcohol at the Health Talk website.

    Just like with drugs or alcohol, there can be pressure from friends or people around you to smoke – again, don’t do something you don’t feel comfortable with and know the risks. Smoking is highly addictive and can cause serious health problems, including cancer. Once you start, it can be really hard to stop. The Childline website has all you need to know about giving up, how to support a friend and all the side effects.

    And we’ve got 7 reasons for you to give up smoking or reduce the amount you smoke:

  • You'll be healthier and less out of breath because smoking decreases your lung capacity.
  • You’ll save yourself a lot of money.
  • You’ll look better. Smokers have more wrinkled and saggy faces by the time they’re in their mid-20s.
  • Quitting smoking helps save the planet.
  • Someone who starts smoking at 15 is three times more likely to die from cancer than someone who starts smoking in their mid-20s.
  • Not smoking will make you instantly more attractive. Most people prefer kissing non- smokers.
  • Smoking can harm your fertility and, if you're female, increases your chances of complications during pregnancy and labour.
